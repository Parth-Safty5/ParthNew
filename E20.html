Summary of "4.4 The Tempes"

Summary of "4.4 The Tempes"

The Betrayal and Exile

Prospero, the Duke of Milan, was a studious man who left the management of his state to his brother, Antonio. Driven by greed, Antonio conspired with Alonso, the King of Naples, to overthrow Prospero. They set Prospero and his baby daughter, Miranda, adrift in a broken boat. Fortunately, a loyal lord named Gonzalo secretly provided them with food, water, and Prospero’s most prized possession—his magic books.


Life on the Enchanted Island

The boat reached an enchanted island that had been under the spell of the witch Sycorax. Prospero used his magic to free good spirits imprisoned in trees, including Ariel, who became his loyal servant. He also encountered the monster Caliban, whom he made his servant. Prospero raised Miranda on the island and spent twelve years perfecting his magical arts.


The Tempest and the Reunion

When Prospero learned that his enemies were sailing near the island, he used his magic to raise a massive storm (a tempest). He ensured no one was harmed but separated the passengers to further his plans:

Ferdinand, the King's son, was led to Prospero’s cave. He and Miranda fell in love at first sight. Prospero tested Ferdinand's love by making him perform hard labor, eventually giving the couple his blessing.

Alonso and Antonio were led through the island, haunted by Ariel’s magic and filled with guilt for their past crimes.


Forgiveness and Resolution

Ariel eventually brought the repentant enemies before Prospero. Clothed in his Duke’s robes, Prospero revealed himself. He forgave Antonio and Alonso, and Alonso was overjoyed to find his son Ferdinand alive and engaged to Miranda.


The story ends with:

• Prospero breaking his magic staff and giving up his powers.

• The group setting sail back to Italy for the wedding.

• Prospero granting Ariel his freedom.

• Prospero returning to Milan to be welcomed back by his people, choosing noble forgiveness over revenge.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1. Prospero forgave Antonio, but do you think their relationship can ever be truly restored? What was the real motive behind this forgiveness?

Ans: It is highly unlikely that their relationship will return to how it was. Antonio showed no clear signs of genuine remorse or apology throughout the story. Prospero’s forgiveness was more about his own inner peace than Antonio’s redemption. By forgiving his brother, Prospero broke the cycle of hatred and revenge that had consumed him for twelve years, allowing him to return to Milan with a clean conscience.

Q2. Why is Prospero’s decision to break his staff and bury his books considered the most important part of his character development?

Ans: In the beginning, Prospero lost his Dukedom because he was too obsessed with his "secret studies" and neglected his duties. By drowning his books and breaking his staff, he shows that he has learned his lesson. He is choosing to rule through human wisdom and responsibility rather than supernatural force. It signifies his return to the real world and his acceptance of his mortality.

Q3. Is Caliban truly a "vicious monster," or is he a victim of colonization?

Ans: This is a modern perspective often missed in basic summaries. Caliban claims, "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother." To him, Prospero is an invader who took his land and enslaved him. While Caliban is depicted as "vicious" because of his behavior, his anger stems from his loss of freedom. He represents the "native" perspective, making the audience question whether Prospero was a teacher or just a conqueror.

Q4. How does Ariel—a non-human spirit—actually teach Prospero how to be a better human?

Ans: This is a great irony in the story. Ariel, who has no human feelings, tells Prospero that his enemies' suffering is so great that even a spirit would feel sorry for them. This observation acts as a mirror for Prospero. He realizes that if a spirit can feel compassion, then he, as a human, must choose mercy over vengeance. Ariel essentially saves Prospero from becoming a tyrant.

Q5. Why did Prospero make the "trials of love" so difficult for Ferdinand by making him carry heavy logs?

Ans: He believed that "too light winning makes the prize light." By forcing a Prince to do the work of a servant (Caliban’s job), Prospero was testing Ferdinand’s humility and his willingness to sacrifice his ego for Miranda. It was a test of character, not just physical strength.

Q6. What does Miranda’s famous line, "O brave new world!", reveal about her character and the theme of the play?

Ans: It reveals her total innocence and lack of experience. It highlights the theme of "Perspective": while Prospero sees the men as sinners who need punishment, Miranda sees them as wonders. It shows her hope for a world she has never known.
