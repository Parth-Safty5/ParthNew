​Step into the ancient world of the Trojan War. This page provides accurate and easy-to-understand solutions for 'The Fall of Troy' English Workshop. From the legendary story of the 'Wooden Horse' to the fall of the great city, we cover every exercise and margin question from your textbook to help you master this epic chapter.

Q.1. Read the following passage and do the activities:

A1. Simple Factual Activity

(1) The Greek leaders made a smart choice by listening to the advice of the wise Odysseus.

(2) The Trojans believed that the long and tiring war was finally coming to an end.

(3) The last person to climb into the wooden horse was the architect who designed it.

(4) Troy wasn t captured through battle it was taken by using a clever trick

 ParthNew

 

A2. Comprehension Factual Activities

(1) Put the following events in the order in which they took place:

1. The Greeks burned their tents and sailed away. = 4

2. The clever Odysseus came up with a plan. = 1

3. The Greeks built a huge wooden horse. = 2

4. The brave Greek leaders hid inside the horse. = 3

5. The Trojans were thrilled to see the Greek ships leave. = 5

  ParthNew

 

(2) How did Odysseus plan to defeat the Trojans?

Odysseus came up with a clever idea: he suggested building a large wooden horse and hiding soldiers inside it. The rest of the Greeks would pretend to leave by burning their tents and sailing away. Then, under the cover of night, they would return and surprise the Trojans.

 

(3) What was the reason for Trojans to rejoice and celebrate?

The Trojans were overjoyed when they saw that the Greek tents were gone and no soldiers were in sight. The shore looked empty, and they believed the long war had finally ended. That s why they celebrated with happiness

  ParthNew

A4. Activities Based on Contextual Grammar

(1) Read the following constructions carefully and then use them to express your ideas:

A5. Personal Response

Was it enough to build a wooden horse to hide some soldiers? What was done to make the Trojans take it inside the city?

→ The Greeks burned their tents and made it look like they had given up and sailed back to Greece. This trick convinced the Trojans that the war was over, making them feel safe enough to bring the wooden horse into their city.

  ParthNew

 

A2. Complex Factual Activities

(1) Put the following events in the order of their occurrence:

1.   The Greek ships had not sailed far. → 1

2.   Nothing of the proud, rich city of Troy was left behind   4

3.   The Greek army entered the city. → 2

4.   Troy was burnt down. → 3

  ParthNew

 

2. How did the Greek ships remain hidden from Troy?

→ The Greeks hadn t gone far from Troy. They anchored their ships behind an island and lowered their sails. The island was positioned behind the wooden horse, making the fleet invisible from the city.

 

3. What did the Greek soldiers hidden in the wooden horse do after entering Troy?

→ As soon as they received the signal from the fleet, the soldiers hidden inside the horse opened the side of the structure and came out, ready to attack.

  ParthNew

 

4. What distressing sounds and sights were witnessed at Troy that fateful night?

→ That night was filled with horror. King Priam was killed along with his brave sons. Hector s wife, his mother, and his sister were also caught in the tragedy, and the city was plunged into chaos and sorrow.

 

5. What was the destiny of the royal family of Troy and that of Helen?

→ King Priam was killed along with all his brave sons. Hector s wife and son were taken away. Helen was brought back to Greece, and the Greeks forgave her. She lived peacefully in Greece afterward.

  ParthNew

Writing Skill

Q. Imagine you are a pair of Trojans and you have come to know about Odysseus's plan. Make a dialogue and act out their plan to your fellow Trojans.

While inspecting the large wooden horse left by the Greeks, I noticed strange movements coming from inside. It felt suspicious, as if someone was hiding within. I immediately realized this could be a trick a secret plan by Odysseus to attack our city during the night. I rushed to inform the others and suggested we tie the horse tightly with ropes so no one could escape. We could keep the horse outside the city walls and guard it closely. If we capture the hidden soldiers, we might force the Greeks to abandon their siege and leave Troy forever.
