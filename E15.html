5. Read the following sentence.

‘A very wise man once remarked that of the unspoken word you are a master, of the spoken word, you are a slave.’

The sentence has two pairs of opposites – spoken and unspoken, and master and slave. The contrasting ideas make the sentence more effective. Putting together opposite or contrasting ideas in one sentence is a literary device. It is called antithesis.

Read the following examples of antithesis.
• Give every man thy ear, but few thy voice.
• Man proposes, God disposes.
• Speech is silver, but silence is gold.
• Patience is bitter, but it has a sweet fruit.

7. We have seen that clauses are parts of a sentence, and they are classified as main and subordinate or dependent clauses. Dependent clauses can be further classified as follows according to the work they do in a sentence.

How do we decide whether a clause is a noun clause, an adjective clause or an adverb clause? There is a simple rule – we can replace an adverb clause with an adverb, an adjective clause with an adjective and a noun clause with a noun.

Look at the following examples:

He told us a funny story. (Adjective)
He told us a story that was funny. (Adjective clause)
They went away. (Adverb)
They went when you were talking to your friend. (Adverb clause)

Summary of "3.4 Think before you Speak"

Summary of "3.4 Think before you Speak"

The lesson "Think Before You Speak" emphasizes the importance of mindfulness and restraint in our speech. It starts with a reflection on the human body's design, noting that nature gave us two ears and only one mouth so we can listen twice as much as we speak. While our ears are always open, our words must pass through two "fences"—the teeth and the lips—before they are released. This suggests that we should think twice before uttering even a single word. Once a word leaves your lips, you lose control over it; you are the master of your unspoken words, but a slave to the ones you have already spoken.

To illustrate how impossible it is to take back hurtful words, the text tells the story of a young man and his wise teacher. After the young man spoke harshly to a friend, the teacher had him write those words on a sheet of paper, tear it into tiny bits, and throw them out of a window. When the teacher asked him to go back and collect all the pieces, the young man found it impossible because the wind had scattered them everywhere. This serves as a powerful metaphor: just like the bits of paper, once harsh words are "scattered" into the world, they can never be fully retrieved or undone, no matter how much we regret them.

Finally, the lesson provides a practical guide through the wisdom of the philosopher Socrates. He advised his disciples to ask themselves three questions before speaking: Is it true? Is it pleasant? And is it useful? Socrates believed that we should only speak if our words are factual, kind, and beneficial to the listener. By filtering our thoughts through these three questions, we can avoid spreading untruths or causing unnecessary pain. Ultimately, the lesson teaches us that silence is often better than speaking in haste or anger.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1: Why does the author use the metaphor of "fences" (teeth and lips) instead of just saying "keep your mouth shut"?

Answer: The metaphor of the "two fences" emphasizes that nature has placed multiple physical barriers to stop words. It suggests that speaking should be a multi-step process: first, the word must pass the "fence" of the teeth, and then the "fence" of the lips. This visual helps the reader realize that speaking requires effort and intention, whereas hearing (through open funnels) is passive.

Q2: In the story of the torn paper, why did the teacher ask the young man to write the words down before tearing them up?

Answer: Writing the words down made the young man confront the "harshness" of what he said. It turned invisible sounds into something visible and physical. By tearing the paper, the teacher showed that while you can "break" or regret your words, the pieces (the consequences) still exist and get scattered by the "wind" of gossip or memory.

Q3: What is the "Slavery of the Spoken Word" mentioned in the text?

Answer: The text says, "Once you have spoken a word, you are a slave to it." This means that once a secret is told or an insult is thrown, you no longer have the power to hide it or take it back. You are now "bound" by the consequences—such as a broken friendship or a ruined reputation—and you must live with the result.

Q4: Is Socrates suggesting we should never say something unpleasant, even if it is true?

Answer: Not necessarily. Socrates uses a triple-filter. If something is unpleasant but true and highly useful (like a doctor giving a diagnosis or a teacher correcting a mistake), it might pass the filter. However, if it is unpleasant, true, but not useful (like pointing out someone’s physical flaw), then Socrates advises silence.

Q5: How does the concept of "Adaptation" from science relate to this English lesson?

Answer: The textbook side-note asks about biological reasons for our features. Evolutionarily, humans developed "funnel-like" ears to catch sound from all directions for survival (detecting predators). However, the mouth evolved for both eating and complex communication. The lesson suggests that while we adapted to hear everything, we must "mentally adapt" to control our complex speech.
