8. Write points and counter points on the following topics:

• Topic 1: You should study all subjects in your mother tongue.
o Points (For): Learning in one's mother tongue makes it easier to understand complex concepts quickly. it also helps in preserving local culture and heritage.
o Counter points (Against): Many higher education resources and global job opportunities require proficiency in international languages like English. Studying only in a mother tongue might limit a student's global reach.
• Topic 2: You must memorise the rules of grammar.
o Points (For): Knowing the rules provides a strong foundation for writing and speaking accurately. It helps avoid misunderstandings and ensures professional communication.
o Counter points (Against): Language is best learned through usage and immersion rather than rote memorization. Focusing too much on rules can make a person hesitant or "stiff" when trying to speak naturally.

9. Why was Franz sad?

Franz was sad because he realized that this was his last French lesson and he would no longer be able to learn his mother tongue. He felt a deep sense of regret for having neglected his studies, wasting time chasing birds' eggs or sliding on the Saar instead of learning his grammar. The sudden realization that his books, which had seemed like such a nuisance before, were now like "old friends" made him feel heavy-hearted.

10. What should people never lose even when they lose freedom?

According to the story, people should never lose their language. M. Hamel explains that when a people are enslaved, as long as they hold fast to their language, it is as if they have the "key to their prison". Language is a symbol of identity, unity, and cultural pride that remains with a person even when physical freedom is taken away.

Summary of "4.5 The Last Lesson"

Summary of "4.5 The Last Lesson"

1. A Strange Morning

The story is told by a young French boy named Franz. One morning, he is late for school and afraid of being scolded by his teacher, M. Hamel, especially since Franz hasn't studied his grammar lesson on participles. As he walks to school, he notices a crowd at the town hall bulletin board, where all the bad news about the war usually appears.

2. An Unusual Atmosphere

When Franz reaches school, he is surprised by the unusual silence. Usually, the school is noisy with the sound of opening desks and lessons being repeated. Instead, it feels as quiet as a Sunday morning. Even more surprising, M. Hamel is dressed in his best clothes—a green coat and silk cap—which he only wears on special days. At the back of the classroom, village elders like the former mayor and postmaster are sitting quietly, looking sad.

3. The Shocking News

M. Hamel stands up and kindly breaks the news: an order has come from Berlin that only German will be taught in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine. This is their very last French lesson.

Franz is shocked. He suddenly regrets all the time he wasted instead of learning his language. His books, which used to feel like a heavy burden, now feel like old friends he doesn't want to leave. He also feels sorry for M. Hamel, who is being forced to leave after forty years of faithful service.

4. The Importance of Language

During the lesson, M. Hamel explains that French is the most beautiful, clearest, and most logical language in the world. He tells the class that they must guard it among themselves because:

"When a people are enslaved, as long as they hold fast to their language, it is as if they had the key to their prison."

Franz finds himself paying more attention than ever before. Everything the teacher says seems easy to understand because both the teacher and the students are finally realizing the true value of their education.

5. A Final Farewell

As the church clock strikes twelve, the sound of Prussian trumpets is heard outside. M. Hamel tries to speak, but he is too overcome with emotion to finish his sentence. Instead, he turns to the blackboard, takes a piece of chalk, and writes as large as he can:

"VIVE LA FRANCE!" (Long Live France!)

He then leans his head against the wall and signals with his hand that school is dismissed.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Why did the Prussian occupation forces decide to ban French in schools immediately?

The ban was a strategic move to undermine the cultural identity of the Alsatians. By removing the French language, the Prussians aimed to sever the historical and emotional connection between the people of Alsace and France, making them easier to assimilate into the German Empire over time.

2. How does the character of M. Hamel represent the "silent resistance" of the local people?

M. Hamel represents resistance not through violence, but through cultural preservation. By teaching with extra devotion on his final day and emphasizing the beauty of the French language, he encourages his students to keep their identity alive despite the foreign occupation. His writing of "Vive La France!" is his final, defiant stand.

. What does the presence of the village elders on the back benches symbolize?

Their presence symbolizes a collective sense of guilt, regret, and patriotism. They represent the older generation who failed to prioritize education for themselves and their children. They are there to pay their respects to the teacher, the language, and the country they are losing, demonstrating that the loss of language affects the entire community, not just the students.

4. In what way does Franz's attitude towards his books change, and what does this reflect?

Initially, Franz views his books as nuisances—heavy, boring burdens. After hearing about the ban, he sees them as "old friends" he cannot abandon. This transformation reflects a universal truth: we only realize the true value of something once we are about to lose it. It highlights the carelessness of youth towards cultural heritage.

5. What is the symbolic meaning of the Prussian soldiers drilling behind the sawmill?

The soldiers drilling symbolize the encroaching power of war and the loss of freedom. Their presence is a constant reminder that the quiet, studious atmosphere of the classroom is being forcefully shattered by the harsh realities of military occupation.
