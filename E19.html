ENGLISH WORKSHOP

1. If you were asked to draw a detailed picture of the scene described in the poem, what object, animals, natural features, etc. will you show in the picture? Make a list.

Objects: Houses, windows, kennel, nests.

Animals: Dogs, doves, mouse, fish.

Natural features: Moon, moonlight, trees, fruits, river, river plants.

2. Write the rhyming words and the rhyme scheme of the poem.

The rhyming words are:

. moon - shoon

· catch - thatch

. peep - sleep

· gleam - stream

. sees - trees

· log - dog

. by - eye.

The rhyme scheme of the poem is aabb.

3. Underline the word silver/silvery in the poem. In which lines does it occur? What pattern does it show?

Silver


Slowly, silently, now the moon

Walks the night in her silver shoon;

This way, and that, she peers, and sees

Silver fruit upon silver trees;

One by one the casements catch

Her beams beneath the silvery thatch;

Couched in his kennel, like a log,

With paws of silver sleeps the dog;

From their shadowy cote the white breasts peep

Of doves in silver feathered sleep

A harvest mouse goes scampering by,

With silver claws, and silver eye;

And moveless fish in the water gleam,

By silver reeds in a silver stream.


The word silver/silvery appears in every alternate line of the poem. The even lines do the colouring of the objects under consideration.

4. Can you think of a parallel scene of dawn/evening when everything is steeped in golden light?

1. Yes, in the evening, the sun sinks below the horizon to set. As it goes lower and lower, its dark-orange-crimson rays spread across the sky and the surroundings steep in gold light. It brings an awesome scenery to view.

2. Dawn is the first appearance of light in the sky before sunrise. The golden glow covering the sky, signals to the animals, especially, the birds that the sun is about to rise.

3. As a result, there is a lot of chirping of the birds like the rooster, the cuckoo, and many more.

4. The birds chirping are like a wake-up call for everyone. There is a golden light covering the sky. The golden light marks the beginning of a new day.

5. The same landscape appears different at different times. What message can we draw from this?

• 'Difference in perception' is what we can mainly draw from this. Life will always offer happiness and difficulties to everyone but the difference lies in how we deal with situations. The same glass can appear half-full to one and half-empty to the other. It all depends on how we perceive things.

• The given line can convey a variety of themes, such as: a single ray of light can eradicate all darkness, exactly as the silvery moonlight does on a gloomy night.

• At the same time, it conveys the concept that an object or person might have numerous qualities or characteristics that emerge in different settings. Light may depict someone or something positively, but darkness may not.

• As a result, we should avoid being lured by flashy 'appearances'. Another perspective is that the same thing may appear different at different times. As a result, people's views of the same thing can differ over time and among themselves.

Summary of "4.3 Silver"

Summary of "4.3 Silver"

1. The Moon’s Journey

The poem begins by personifying the moon as a lady wearing "silver shoon" (shoes). She moves slowly and silently across the night sky, peering down at the world below. Everything she touches with her light is instantly transformed.


2. A Silver Transformation

The poet describes various objects and creatures as they are bathed in moonlight. The repetition of the word "silver" emphasizes the uniform, metallic glow covering the earth:

Plants: The fruit and trees turn silver.

Architecture: The "casements" (windows) and "thatch" (roofs) catch the moonbeams.

Animals: A dog sleeping in its kennel, doves in their "cote" (shelter), and a harvest mouse scampering by are all described as having silver features (paws, feathers, claws, and eyes).

Water: Even the fish and the reeds in the stream gleam with a silver light.


3. Themes and Atmosphere

Peace and Stillness: The poem creates a sense of absolute quiet. Most of the subjects (the dog, the doves, the fish) are motionless or sleeping, contributing to the "stilly" atmosphere of the night.

Magic in the Ordinary: De la Mare takes everyday objects—a dog, a mouse, a window—and makes them seem ethereal and extraordinary through the lens of the moon.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Why does the poet use the word "shoon" instead of "shoes"?

The word "shoon" is an archaic (old-fashioned) plural for shoes. By using it, the poet gives the moon a timeless, fairy-tale quality. It also creates a perfect rhyme with "moon," establishing a rhythmic, hypnotic tone that mimics a slow, silent walk.

2. Does the moon actually change the color of the animals?

No. This is an example of visual imagery. The moon acts like a silver "filter" or a "spotlight." By describing the dog’s paws and the mouse’s claws as silver, the poet is showing how the moonlight is so intense and uniform that it washes away the natural colors of the world, making everything look like it's made of the same precious metal.

3. Why are all the creatures in the poem sleeping or "moveless"?

Except for the "scampering" mouse, every creature (the dog, the doves, the fish) is still. This emphasizes the absolute silence of the night. The stillness of the animals reflects the stillness of the moon’s movement, creating a sense of a world frozen in a beautiful, silvery moment.

4. What is the significance of the "silver reeds in a silver stream"?

In the final line, the poet uses repetition to show that there is no boundary between the water and the plants at night. Under the moonlight, the reflection on the water and the physical plants look identical. It suggests that nature becomes one unified, glowing entity during the night.

5. How does the poem use "Personification" differently than other poems?

Most poems personify the moon as a goddess who speaks or acts. In "Silver," the moon is personified as a silent observer. She doesn't speak; she only "peers and sees." This makes her feel like a protective or ghostly figure watching over the sleeping world without disturbing it.
