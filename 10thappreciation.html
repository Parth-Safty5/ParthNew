Unit-wise Solved Poems

1.1 A Teenager's Prayer

Poet:

J. Morse

Rhyme Scheme:

abcb

Figure of Speech:

Apostrophe, Metaphor, Alliteration.

Central Idea:

This is a teenager's prayer to God for guidance. The speaker asks for help to make the right decisions in life, keep the mind clean, and stay away from temptations during the formative years.

1.3 Basketful of Moonlight

Poet:

Sunil Sharma

Rhyme Scheme:

Free Verse (No regular rhyme scheme)

Figure of Speech:

Alliteration, Repetition, Personification.

Central Idea:

The poem highlights a child's innocent wish to help his village. He requests the moon for seeds of moonlight to light up the dark path from the city, so his father and other villagers can return home safely and early.

2.1 You Start Dying Slowly

Poet:

Pablo Neruda

Rhyme Scheme:

Free Verse

Figure of Speech:

Repetition, Antithesis, Metaphor.

Central Idea:

The poem is a warning against living a life of routine without passion. To truly live, one must travel, read, take risks, and follow their dreams instead of "dying slowly" in a comfort zone.

2.4 The Twins

Poet:

Henry S. Leigh

Rhyme Scheme:

ababcdcd

Figure of Speech:

Hyperbole, Pun, Alliteration.

Central Idea:

A humorous and light-hearted poem that describes the extreme confusion caused by the identical appearance of twins. It uses exaggeration (hyperbole) to show how they were mistaken for each other from birth to death.

3.1 If...

Poet:

Rudyard Kipling

Rhyme Scheme:

Stanza 1: aaaa; Others: abab

Figure of Speech:

Personification, Antithesis, Metaphor.

Central Idea:

This didactic poem provides a set of rules for character building. It advises how to maintain balance, integrity, and stoicism in the face of both success and failure to become a "Man".

3.3 Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening

Poet:

Robert Frost

Rhyme Scheme:

aaba

Figure of Speech:

Alliteration, Inversion, Personification.

Central Idea:

The poet is captivated by the beauty of nature but realizes that he cannot stay. It symbolizes the conflict between the desire for rest and the obligations one must fulfill in life before death.

4.1 The World is Mine

Poet:

Joy Lovelet Crawford

Rhyme Scheme:

Irregular / Free Verse

Figure of Speech:

Apostrophe, Inversion, Metaphor.

Central Idea:

The poem teaches us the value of gratitude. We often complain about small things, but the poet reminds us that if we have our health and senses, we are truly blessed and the "world is ours".

4.3 O Captain! My Captain!

Poet:

Walt Whitman

Rhyme Scheme:

aabbcded

Figure of Speech:

Extended Metaphor, Synecdoche, Personification.

Central Idea:

An elegy written on the death of Abraham Lincoln. It uses the metaphor of a ship returning from a voyage to describe the end of the Civil War and the tragic loss of the nation's leader.
