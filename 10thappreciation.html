1.1 A Teenager's Prayer
Poet: J. Morse Rhyme Scheme: abcb Figure of Speech: Apostrophe, Metaphor, Alliteration. Central Idea: A teenager's prayer to God for guidance. The child asks for help to make the right decisions and stay away from life's temptations.
1.3 Basketful of Moonlight
Poet: Sunil Sharma Rhyme Scheme: Free Verse (No rhyme scheme) Figure of Speech: Alliteration, Repetition, Personification. Central Idea: A child's innocent request to the moon for seeds of light to brighten the dark path to his village, so his father can return home early.
2.1 You Start Dying Slowly
Poet: Pablo Neruda Rhyme Scheme: Free Verse Figure of Speech: Repetition, Antithesis, Metaphor. Central Idea: This poem warns us against leadings a boring life. We must travel, read, and listen to the sounds of life to truly live.
2.4 The Twins
Poet: Henry S. Leigh Rhyme Scheme: ababcdcd Figure of Speech: Hyperbole, Pun, Alliteration. Central Idea: A humorous poem about the confusion and absurd situations faced by identical twins throughout their lives.
3.1 If...
Poet: Rudyard Kipling Rhyme Scheme: Stanza 1: aaaa, Others: abab Figure of Speech: Personification, Antithesis, Metaphor. Central Idea: Advice from a father to a son on how to be a balanced human being by maintaining self-belief and treating success and failure equally.
3.3 Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening
Poet: Robert Frost Rhyme Scheme: aaba Figure of Speech: Alliteration, Inversion, Personification. Central Idea: The poet is tempted to stay in the beautiful woods, but he remembers he has many responsibilities and promises to fulfill before he dies.
4.1 The World is Mine
Poet: Joy Lovelet Crawford Rhyme Scheme: Irregular / Free Verse Figure of Speech: Apostrophe, Inversion, Metaphor. Central Idea: A message of gratitude. It teaches us to be thankful for our health and senses rather than complaining about what we don't have.
4.3 O Captain! My Captain!
Poet: Walt Whitman Rhyme Scheme: aabbcded Figure of Speech: Extended Metaphor, Synecdoche, Personification. Central Idea: An elegy mourning the death of Abraham Lincoln. It contrasts the victory of the nation with the personal grief of losing a great leader.
